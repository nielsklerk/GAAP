\chapter{Data Description}

Euclid is a space telescope developed \gls{esa} and was launched in 2023. The goal of Euclid is to study the dark energy and dark matter content in the universe, as well as the large-scale structure and weak lensing. To achieve this, it uses optical and near-infrared imaging in combination with spectroscopy.


\section{Euclid Deep Field}

In this work, data from Euclid from \gls{q1} is used. Specifically \gls{edf}, which is divided into 3 parts: \gls{edfn}, \gls{edff}, and \gls{edfs} (\autoref{fig: covarage}. The deep fields consist of a total of 50 deg$^2$. Each of the three regions is visited multiple times for observations. The goal of the deep fields is to observe faint and distant objects in the universe

\section{Euclid Instruments}

On board Euclid, 2 instruments take observations using the \gls{vis} and \gls{nisp} instruments, which operate in the visible part of the spectrum and the near-infrared, respectively. \gls{vis} contains a single filter, whereas \gls{nisp} has three filters


\section{MER Mosaic}

The \glspl{edf} are observed using the two instruments. Different observations are stacked to create \gls{mer} mosaics. In addition to the filters from the \gls{vis} and \gls{nisp}, complementary ground-based telescopes are used, namely: \gls{cfht}, \gls{hsc}, \gls{panstarrs}, and \gls{decam}. The observations with the external telescopes are part of the \gls{cfis}, \gls{wishes}/\gls{whigs}, \gls{panstarrs}, and \gls{des}, respectively. These improve the wavelength coverage. All of the observations are resampled and rescaled to a common size in such a way that the images in the different fields all have a pixel scale of 0.1"\footnote{1"=1/3600$^\circ$}.

These filters cover the wavelength range from $\sim0.4\mathrm{\mu m}$ to $\sim1.7\mathrm{\mu m}$, giving information about the light emitted by sources in the optical and near-infrared.
In this work, the \gls{mer} mosaics are used that have been background subtracted. \textbf{ZEROPOINTS CORRECTION} In addition to the mosaics, the \gls{rms} maps and \gls{psf} grids are used. The \gls{rms} maps capture the error in the pixel values of the observations and are used to compute the error on the flux. The \gls{psf} grids are, as the name suggests, a grid of \glspl{psf}, which can be used to interpolate the shape of the \gls{psf} depending on the location in the image.

\section{MER Catalog}

The \gls{mer} catalogs are complementary to the \gls{mer} mosaics. They contain information about the sources in each observation. They contain the coordinates of the sources, with information about the \gls{fwhm}, flux, morphology, etc. In this work, the coordinates and \gls{fwhm} are used to calculate the aperture flux using \gls{pca-phot}. The results of this technique are compared to the colors derived from the flux measurements in the catalog.
\newline
The \gls{mer} mosaics, \gls{mer} catalogs, \gls{rms} maps, and \gls{psf} grids are retrieved from the Euclid Science Archive\footnote{\url{https://eas.esac.esa.int/sas/}}, where they are publicly available.
